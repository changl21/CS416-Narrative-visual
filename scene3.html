1.General Trend of Increasing Labor Productivity:Almost all countries show an upward trend in labor productivity.This is likely due to the technological advancements and economic recovery post-World War II. The rapid industrialization and modernization of economies globally contributed to this increase.

2.Steady Growth in Europe and North America: European and North American countries exhibit a steady and continuous increase in labor productivity.These regions benefited from strong institutional frameworks, significant investments in technology and education, and stable economic policies that supported sustained growth.

3.Varied Growth in Asia and Africa:The growth trends in Asia and Africa vary widely. Some countries show rapid increases, while others have minimal changes in productivity.The differences can be attributed to varying levels of industrialization, access to technology, and economic policies. For example, some Asian countries rapidly industrialized and integrated into the global economy, while others remained more agrarian.

4.Rapid Growth in China Post-Reform and Opening-Up:China shows a particularly rapid increase in labor productivity, especially after the late 1970s.The economic reforms and opening-up policies initiated by China in 1978 led to significant economic transformations. These policies promoted market-oriented reforms, foreign investments, and industrialization, which substantially boosted productivity.

5.Moderate Growth in Vietnam:Vietnam's growth in labor productivity is not as pronounced as China's but still shows improvement. While Vietnam also implemented economic reforms (Doi Moi) starting in 1986, the scale and impact were less dramatic compared to China's reforms. However, Vietnam has steadily improved its productivity through gradual industrialization and integration into the global economy.

Based on the analysis, global labor productivity has generally shown an upward trend since World War II, primarily due to technological advancements and economic recovery. Rapid industrialization and modernization of economies have been key drivers of this growth. In Europe and North America, labor productivity has steadily and continuously increased, benefiting from strong institutional frameworks, significant investments in technology and education, and stable economic policies.

In contrast, the growth trends in Asia and Africa vary significantly. Some countries have shown rapid increases in productivity, while others have seen minimal changes. These differences can be attributed to varying levels of industrialization, access to technology, and economic policies.

China, in particular, has experienced a rapid increase in labor productivity, especially after the economic reforms and opening-up policies initiated in the late 1970s. These policies promoted market-oriented reforms, foreign investments, and industrialization, significantly boosting productivity. Vietnam's growth in labor productivity, while not as pronounced as China's, still shows improvement. Although Vietnam also implemented economic reforms starting in 1986, the scale and impact were less dramatic compared to China's reforms. However, through gradual industrialization and integration into the global economy, Vietnam's productivity has steadily improved.

Summary: What We can Learn

The distributions of labor productivity, average working hours, and GDP are highly uneven across the world, reflecting varying levels of economic development. Workers in poorer countries, which have lower GDP or labor productivity, tend to work more hours than their counterparts in wealthier nations. This disparity means that residents in countries like Myanmar and Cambodia are not only financially poorer but also have less leisure time, as their economies, characterized by low productivity, require longer working hours.

Technological advancement and economic globalization are key drivers of labor productivity growth, which eventually lead to shorter average working hours. However, the extent to which different countries benefit from these factors varies significantly, exacerbating global economic inequality. For example, Europe and North America exhibit steady and continuous increases in labor productivity due to strong institutional frameworks, significant investments in technology and education, and stable economic policies. In contrast, Asia and Africa show more varied growth patterns. Some countries in these regions experience rapid productivity increases, while others see minimal changes, depending on their levels of industrialization, access to technology, and economic policies.

This set of data prompts a reconsideration of our understanding of economic globalization and wealth distribution over the past century, highlighting significant economic and work inequalities among different countries and regions. Despite the positive correlation between productivity and GDP, high productivity does not always equate to the highest GDP per capita, as other factors such as economic policies, natural resources, and market conditions play crucial roles.

The analysis also reveals that countries with higher productivity generally have shorter working hours, similar to trends observed in GDP per capita. Over time, global productivity has increased, reflecting overall economic growth and improved labor efficiency due to technological advancements, better education, and more effective economic policies. However, long-standing economic structures and development levels mean that Europe and North America are consistently in the high productivity, low working hours quadrant, while Asia and Africa are more often in the low productivity, high working hours quadrant.

These insights underscore the importance of labor productivity and efficient economic structures in achieving economic growth and high living standards. Policymakers can use this data to develop strategies that enhance labor productivity, optimize working hours, and improve workforce skills through education and training. Investing in advanced technologies and education, coupled with ongoing policy reforms in economic liberalization and market openness, is essential for boosting productivity and achieving sustainable economic development. By understanding these long-term trends and the factors driving them, we can inform the development of policies aimed at reducing economic disparities and promoting more equitable growth globally.
