Productivity vs. Working Hours:

In the previous slide, we mentioned the relationship between working hours and GDP, and we found that working hours and GDP are not positively correlated. It has to do with a lot of things including policy, the structure of the economy, productivity. The scatterpolt of worked hour vs productivity discusses the relationship between productivity and working hours. according to the scatterplot, we can get some information directly:

1. Similarity in Distribution with Hours and GDP per Capita:The scatterplot's distribution structure closely resembles the relationship between working hours and GDP per capita. Countries with higher productivity generally have shorter working hours, mirroring the trends seen in the GDP per capita data. The reason might be countries with efficient economic structures and high labor productivity can achieve significant economic output with fewer working hours.

2. Increase in Global Productivity Over Time:There is a noticeable increase in global productivity as the years progress, indicating overall economic growth and improvements in labor efficiency worldwide. It might cause by Technological advancements, better education, and improved economic policies contribute to this upward trend in productivity.

3. Positive Correlation Between Productivity and GDP:Although there is a positive correlation between productivity and GDP, high productivity does not always equate to the highest GDP per capita. For instance, while the United States has high productivity, it does not always have the highest GDP per capita compared to some other countries. This highlights that while productivity is a significant factor, other elements such as economic policies, natural resources, and market conditions also play crucial roles in determining a country's GDP per capita.

4. Consistent Distribution Patterns with Hours and GDP per Capita:Throughout the years from 1970 to 2019, the distribution patterns in the scatterplots of working hours and productivity remain consistent with those of working hours and GDP per capita. Europe and North America are typically positioned in the high productivity, low working hours quadrant, while Asia and Africa are more often found in the low productivity, high working hours quadrant. This consistent distribution reflects long-standing economic structures and development levels in these regions. Europe's and North America's advanced technologies, robust economic policies, and high levels of education contribute to their high productivity. In contrast, many Asian and African countries rely on labor-intensive industries and have less access to advanced technologies, resulting in lower productivity.
